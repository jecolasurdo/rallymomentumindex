(Gene Wang/The Washington Post)By Gene Wang closeGene WangSports reporter covering local and national college football and basketballEmailEmailBioBioFollowFollowReporterJuly 22, 2016 at 11:56 PM EDTThe Washington Mystics on Friday became the latest WNBA team in recent days to strike a defiant tone against the league with a public showing of concern over recent police shootings.Mystics players wore "Black Lives Matter” T-shirts as they entered Verizon Center for their game on Friday night against the Los Angeles Sparks and indicated they would continue voice opinions on social issues despite the league fining teams and players that have made similar gestures.Support our journalism. Subscribe today.arrow-right"The deaths that have occurred from police brutality over the years, it's not going anywhere,” Mystics starting point guard Natasha Cloud said before the game. "For us, this is our season, this is our platform, so we're going to continue to use it whether the WNBA supports us or not.”ADThe WNBA fined the New York Liberty, Indiana Fever and Phoenix Mercury as well as their players on Thursday for wearing black warmup shirts before games in the wake of the recent shootings by and against police officers. Each organization received a $5,000 fine, and players from each team were assessed fines of $500 for violating league rules stating uniforms cannot be altered in any way.ADWhen the Liberty and Fever played each other later Thursday, players from both teams told members of the media they would not answer questions relating to basketball.WNBA players refuse to talk basketball in protest of fines for black warmup shirtsThe Mystics wore regulation WNBA apparel — black shirts with the team name on the front — for pregame warmups and during the national anthem. They normally wear red-and-blue warmups for pregame.AD"I definitely love the way that it was conducted,” Sparks forward Nneka Ogwumike, a top MVP contender, said after scoring 24 points with 14 rebounds in the Los Angeles Sparks' 95-75 win. "It couldn't have been more professional, more unified, and I'm very proud to be a part of this league.”At Target Center in Minneapolis, where the reigning WNBA champion Minnesota Lynx were playing the Seattle Storm on Friday night, fans began chanting "Black lives matter” when Storm rookie sensation Breanna Stewart was attempting free throws. The hashtag #BlackOutLynx also was trending on Twitter during the game with an announced attendance of 12,124.ADIvory Latta, the Mystics' longest-tenured player and a vocal leader in the locker room, spoke emotionally about having to explain to her nephew why he wasn't allowed out of the house past a certain hour. Latta is among the 70 percent of players in the WNBA who are African American, and she reiterated unwavering support for "Black Lives Matter,” which began as a social media hashtag three years ago and has grown into a movement campaigning against violence toward African Americans, particularly at the hands of law enforcement.AD"I'm getting goosebumps because it just needs to stop,” Latta said. "We want the support for what we believe in, not what everybody else wants us to believe in, and that's what I stand for, and I know that everybody [in the Mystics locker room] stands for the same. My inspiration came from Carmelo Anthony.”NBA superstars issue 'call to action' for fellow athletes in appearance at ESPYsThe New York Knicks all-star forward has taken a more visible role lately with regard to social issues, including joining LeBron James, Dwyane Wade and Chris Paul on stage at the ESPY Awards last week in Los Angeles urging more advocacy among all professional athletes.ADEarlier Friday, Anthony took exception to the financial penalties levied against WNBA players for taking a more proactive stance on social issues, saying: "I don't see no reason to fine them. If anything, you should want to support them.”ADThe Liberty has worn plain black warmup shirts four times, including before tip-off of Wednesday afternoon's game against the Mystics at Verizon Center. The Fever and Mercury wore them during their respective games on Tuesday night.On Thursday night after the game between the Liberty and Fever, New York's Swin Cash made a brief statement about the game with teammates gathered behind her in the locker room. Tanisha Wright then spoke about the fines, expressing disappointment in the league for not standing behind the players.Liberty players had worn black T-shirts emblazoned in white lettering with the Twitter hashtags "#BlackLivesMatter” and "#Dallas5” during pregame warmups.ADADEarlier Thursday, Liberty forward Tina Charles accepted the WNBA's player of the month award with her official warmup worn inside-out to make it resemble a black shirt.On July 9, members of the Lynx wore black T-shirts with "Change Starts With Us” in large white lettering and "Justice & Accountability” smaller on the front during warmups at Target Center.The back of the shirts had the names of Philando Castile and Alton Sterling, the two black men killed by law enforcement officials in Falcon Heights, Minn., and Baton Rouge, La., respectively, that stirred protests across the nation. On the back of those shirts was "Black Lives Matter.”Four Minneapolis police officers scheduled to work security for the game walked off the job in protest of the players' message.ADNBA players were not fined when they wore "I Can't Breathe” shirts two years ago during pregame warmups. The shirts were a reference to the final words Eric Garner, who was African American, spoke before he died after an arresting police officer applied a chokehold.AD"I think it's an interesting subject obviously,” said Sparks forward-center Candace Parker, one of the most high-profile women's basketball players in the world. "In other leagues they haven't been fined, and I think that's my question. Where's the line? I understand rules, but I think it should be the same across the board for women and men."I definitely think as athletes, you have to have to speak up for what you believe in and use our profession as a platform.”ADToday's HeadlinesThe most important news stories of the day, curated by Post editors and delivered every morning.By signing up you agree to our Terms of Use and Privacy PolicyComments are not available on this story.Share your feedback by emailing the author.
