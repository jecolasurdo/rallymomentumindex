"He's not (a threat). He's a human being like everyone else. ... He would never deserve to be treated the way they treated George Floyd, or these kids and men who were killed because of the color of their skin."Floyd, 46, died last week in Minneapolis after a white police officer kneeled on his neck for more than eight minutes. Some of his final words, caught on camera, were "I can't breathe." Those words have become a rallying cry against racism and police brutality, not only at Long Branch's march, but across the United States.Buy PhotoDerek Drummond, Long Branch, (center) one of the organizers of protest against police brutality in the wake of the George Floyd killing. Connolly)Floyd's death followed other high-profile killings of African-Americans, including the fatal police shooting of Breonna Taylor in Louisville; the killing of Ahmaud Arbery in Georgia by two white men; and the death Tony McDade, a black transgender man, who was killed by police officers in Tallahassee.Although some rallies across the nation pitted police against protesters and devolved into violence, Long Branch's rally was held with the help of city police. Officers provided an escort for the marchers and traffic control as they made their way through Long Branch to police headquarters.
