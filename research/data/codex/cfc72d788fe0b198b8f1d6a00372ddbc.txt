(Rob Dicker / Chicago Tribune) More than 400 protesters marched through the streets of Grayslake on Thursday afternoon, decrying racism and police actions in the deaths of African Americans in several recent tragedies across the country. Chanting the names of George Floyd, Breonna Taylor and Laquan McDonald, the crowd stretched about a half-mile and shut down Illinois Route 83 and the entrance to the College of Lake County. Advertisement The march was one of many similar events held throughout the suburbs this week that have drawn large crowds. Many of the protesters in Grayslake were students from local schools. The protesters, some of whom wore red shirts, carried signs reading, "Black Lives Matter" and chanted, "No justice, no peace, no racist police.” Advertisement "I'm out here because I'm tired of seeing people who look like me getting killed across the country," said Jeremy Glende, 21, of Gurnee. Sitting in the middle of Washington Street in Grayslake, Lucinda Smetana, of Grayslake, leads eight minutes of silence in memory of George Floyd on June 4, 2020. Hundreds of protesters marched through downtown Grayslake, then headed north to hold a sit-in on Washington Street at the entrance to the College of Lake County. (Rob Dicker / Chicago Tribune) Harper Fischer, a social studies teacher at Grayslake North High School, said she joined the crowd because, "I feel I can't look my students in the face and tell them they're safe in America." Members of Minority Rule, a book club at the school, helped to organize the peaceful event. This school year, Fischer said, the school started an African American social club called the Black Union, which is open to all students. The crowd marched from downtown Grayslake to the College of Lake County, where they occupied the intersection at the entrance, and observed eight minutes and 46 seconds of silence in recognition of the time that a Minneapolis police officer knelt on Floyd's neck, leading to his death. Lainey Hutchcraft, 27, of Lake Villa, said her great-grandfather and grandmother were Chicago police officers, and her cousins married African Americans. But when one officer is guilty of misconduct, and the others keep silent, she said, all those involved become "bad cops” She brought her 1-year-old son and 3-year-old daughter, she said, to show them how people can unite for a just cause. Protesters head east on Center Street toward Illinois Route 83 in Grayslake on June 4, 2020. (Rob Dicker / Chicago Tribune) Speakers at the protest made a point of thanking the police for blocking traffic for them. "The cops back there, we love you too,” said Noah Polk, 20, of Grayslake. "They could take care of us the whole way.” [Most read] Live updates: Thousands gather in downtown Chicago for Juneteenth marches and celebrations » Grayslake police Chief Phil Perlini said the event was peaceful, though a couple of people needed medical attention due to the heat. Advertisement "Everybody was respectful and peaceful, and so were we," he said. While it was hard to hear some of the comments, he said, "Their points do not go unheard." In Lake Zurich's Breezewald Park, a rally organized by high school and college students drew more than 300 people in support of the Black Lives Matter movement and to protest Floyd's death. Students from Lake Zurich High School said they wanted to stand in solidarity with black victims of oppression such as Floyd. [Most read] Field Museum scientist and 'inspiration' Lynika Strozier, 35, dies from coronavirus » They emphasized they wanted a peaceful protest in contrast to the rioting and looting that occurred following protests elsewhere. "It's just as important to educate white people they need to use their voices to stand against oppression of black people," said co-organizer Zach Richards, 17, who is just graduating from high school. The parents of 15-year-old Dana Fleming wouldn't let her attend a protest in Chicago, so she decided to organize her own event. She said it's important for people with white privilege to "stand up for people of color." Isabelle Jordan, 19, said her research showed there was a Ku Klux Klan rally in the area in 1921, suggesting a racist history in the predominantly white area. Along with students and families, some older residents also showed their support. Octogenarians Beryl Ibbotson and Bill Ditman said they hoped the Minneapolis incident would bring about change.
