Residents of all ages and races gathered to call for an end to police brutality and demand justice for George Floyd, who was killed by Minneapolis Police Officer Derek Chauvin earlier this week.Hundreds lined up their cars on Saturday in Morristown to demand justice for George Floyd and an end police brutality. (Photo: Edward Correa)"The response yesterday was so overwhelming with love," said Kimbrough, whose team organized the event.Hundreds of cars stretched from the packed Morristown High School parking lot up to the Green, coordinated in part by Morristown nonprofit Wind of the Spirit.Police escorted the procession through Morristown and onto Woodland Avenue in Morris Township. They continued in a "funeral procession" to the Morris Township police department and a candlelight vigil at Ginty Field.Cars displayed signs calling for justice for Floyd as well as his last words before his death, "I can't breathe." Hundreds of residents drove through Morristown to demand justice for George Floyd after he died at the hands of Minneapolis Police Officer Derek Chauvin. (Photo: Kisha Pinnock)The death of Floyd on Monday in Minneapolis sparked a firestorm of protests and riots throughout the country, with scenes of violence unfolding on social media and TV screens. But in Morristown, and throughout North Jersey, the scenes were peaceful. "The events that led to George Floyd's death are glaringly clear. All over America, we are mourning. We are mourning the life of too many lives lost too for no other reason than they are people of color. We are scared, we are tired and we are grieving," said Kisha Pinnock, an attorney and social justice activist who participated in the procession.Hundreds of Morris County residents gathered in Morristown on Saturday to demand justice for George Floyd after he died at the hands of Minneapolis Police Officer Derek Chauvin. (Photo: Edward Correa)Marchers began chanting, "Say his name, George Floyd," as they walked to Ginty Field. Addressing the marchers, Kimbrough kept the crowd energized.She said, "In Morristown, we're doing it right." Hundreds of Morris County residents drove through Morristown on Saturday to demand justice for George Floyd after he died at the hands of Minneapolis Police Officer Derek Chauvin. (Photo: Edward Correa)At Ginty Field, the crowd swelled and spread across both sides of Woodland Avenue. Demonstrators wore masks and tried to maintain social distancing guidelines. Candles and flashlights lit up the field as nearly 300 people came to show their support. As she addressed the crowd, Kimbrough recalled previous instances of police brutality against the black community. She said she was "tired" as she remembered the killings of Alton Sterling, Philando Castile and Floyd. The evening continued with speakers Rabbi Allison Miller of Morristown Unitarian Fellowship and Pastor Charles Perez of the United Methodist Church. Local residents lined up their cars for a funeral procession in Morristown demanding justice for George Floyd and an end to police brutality. (Photo: Edward Correa)In Englewood, more than 1,000 protestors marched as organizers urged everyone to keep things peaceful. At least 5,000 people gathered around a statue of Abraham Lincoln at the foot of Newark's historic courthouse."I am proud that the citizens of the greater Morristown area, people across all races came together in solidarity," Pinnock said. "It filled my heart. It is not enough to not be racist. We need people who are not of color to be anti-racist and that is what I saw on display."
