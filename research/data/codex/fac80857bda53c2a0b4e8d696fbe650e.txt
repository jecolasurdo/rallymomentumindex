Black Lives Matter rally takes stand against bigotry in Port HuronBob Gross, Times Herald Published 10:59 a.m. ET Aug. 19, 2017 | Updated 3:09 p.m. ET Aug. 19, 2017Buy PhotoAutumn Hinojosa and her son, Braylin, 1, were at a peace march Saturday, Aug. 19, 2017, in Port Huron.(Photo: Bob Gross, Times Herald)Buy PhotoCONNECTTWEETLINKEDINCOMMENTEMAILMOREAutumn Hinojosa said she and her boyfriend, Robert Standfield, organized a Black Lives Matter peace march Saturday in Port Huron to draw attention to racism in the area."We had noticed the Charlottesville riots and the attention they were getting," she said."We know there is a lot of racism close to home, and we wanted to show unity. We wanted to show when our nation is being divided, not everyone is choosing sides."More: Hayes: Where the real blame lies in nation's racial discordGroup will have environmental rally Sunday in parkRiver walk cleanup is MondayHinojosa and Standfield were at the march, which began at Knox Field and continued along 24th Street, with their year-old son, Braylin."We're basically pointing out the obvious," Standfield said. "... We never did say that all lives don't matter, we have never discriminated, but black lives are under fire."Violence at a white nationalist rally in Charlottesville on Aug. 12 left one woman, Heather Heyer, 32, dead and 19 people injured when a car slammed into a crowd of counter-protesters.Buy PhotoAlphonso Amos holds a sign during a protest march in Port Huron on Saturday, Aug. 19, 2017. (Photo: Bob Gross, Times Herald)Jessica Hanks, of Lexington, was carrying a sign expressing her outrage at what happened in Charlottesville."I believe in standing up for oppressed groups," she said. "I feel our voices have to be louder than ever before. We literally have Nazis on the street."Community organizer and former Port Huron Councilman Alphonso Amos said he was marching for equality. He said people need to "make a stand against hatred and bigotry."Buy PhotoParticipants in a peace march hold signs Saturday, Aug. 19, 2017, in Port Huron. (Photo: Bob Gross, Times Herald)Janet Chetcuti was at the march with her son, Daniel. She's from Kimball Township."We're here to make a statement against the current climate in our society, that we cannot tolerate racism, bigotry and discrimination," she said. "We have to stand up for our neighbors."Contact Bob Gross at (810) 989-6263 or rgross@gannett.com.
